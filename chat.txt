‚Źļ Yeah, let me strip the jargon out completely.

  The plain-English version

  Imagine someone borrows money from a bank and puts up their house as
  collateral. If they stop paying, the bank has to seize the house and auction
  it off to recover the loan. That auction is the "liquidation."

  Now imagine two problems with how that auction works today in crypto:

  Problem 1: Everyone can see every bid before the auction ends.
  It's like an auction where every bidder has to write their offer on a giant
  billboard in the town square before the hammer falls. Obviously this is
  stupid. Bidders watch each other, undercut each other, and the seller (the
  borrower whose collateral is being seized) ends up getting a worse price than
  they should. In crypto, professional bots do this at machine speed and squeeze
   the borrower for every cent.

  The fix: sealed envelopes. Everyone writes their bid on paper, drops it in a
  locked box, and only when the deadline hits does someone open the box and
  announce the winner. Nobody ever sees the losing bids. This is what Encrypt
  lets us do ‚ÄĒ except instead of paper envelopes, the bids are cryptographically
   sealed, and the "open the box" step happens automatically.

  Problem 2: The collateral is on the wrong chain.
  Say the borrower put up Bitcoin as collateral, but the loan happens on Solana
  (a different blockchain). Bitcoin and Solana don't natively talk to each
  other. So today, people use something called a "bridge" ‚ÄĒ basically a
  middleman service that locks your real Bitcoin and gives you a fake "wrapped"
  Bitcoin on Solana that represents it. Bridges are the most hacked thing in
  crypto history. Billions of dollars lost. And the worst time for a bridge to
  get hacked is exactly when you're trying to seize collateral, because the
  value is right there waiting to be stolen.

  The fix: don't use a bridge. Let the real Bitcoin stay on Bitcoin, but give
  Solana the ability to control it directly when needed. This is what Ika does ‚ÄĒ
   it's like giving Solana a remote control for assets that live on other
  blockchains, without ever moving them.

  Put both fixes together and you get: a system where someone can borrow against
   their real Bitcoin (which stays on Bitcoin the whole time), and if they
  default, the seizure happens through a sealed-envelope auction where nobody
  can cheat each other or front-run the borrower.

  That's the whole idea.

  A real-world story

  Meet Sarah. Sarah runs a small crypto fund. She owns 10 Bitcoin (worth about
  $700K right now). She doesn't want to sell her Bitcoin because she thinks it's
   going up ‚ÄĒ but she needs $300K cash this month to pay her team and rent.

  Today, her options are bad:

  - Option A: Sell some Bitcoin. She loses her upside if BTC goes up.
  - Option B: Use an existing crypto loan service. She has to send her real
  Bitcoin into a bridge, get a fake "wrapped" Bitcoin in return, deposit that
  into a lending app, and borrow against it. Now her Bitcoin sits inside a
  bridge that could get hacked (and many have). She's added a giant trust
  assumption to a simple loan.
  - Option C: Use a centralized service like a crypto exchange. Now she's
  trusting a company with her Bitcoin. See: FTX, Celsius, BlockFi, etc.

  With our primitive, Sarah's flow is:

  1. Sarah's 10 Bitcoin stays on Bitcoin. It never leaves. It just gets put into
   a special address that she co-controls with a network of computers (the
  dWallet). To move it, both Sarah AND the network have to agree.
  2. Sarah borrows $300K USDC on Solana against that Bitcoin. The loan is
  recorded on Solana but the Bitcoin itself never moved.
  3. Sarah uses the cash, runs her business, pays her team.

  Now imagine Bitcoin crashes 40% suddenly. Sarah's collateral isn't worth
  enough to cover the loan anymore. The system needs to seize and sell some of
  her Bitcoin to repay the lenders.

  Today's bad version of liquidation:
  - The seizure goes on a public order book
  - 50 trading bots see it instantly
